Slavery in Late-19th-Century Siam

A portfolio-level data science reconstruction, 1850–1905, with a focus on Lao / Isan exploitation.

This project reconstructs slavery in Siam as a measurable system of labor extraction, debt, war captivity, and ethnic classification rather than as a purely legal institution. Using a manually coded dataset built from 10 historical sources and 252 observation rows, the analysis estimates plausible slave-stock ranges, Lao captive totals, debt-redemption burdens, census omission shares, and the relationship between abolition, administrative reform, and Bangkok-centered state formation.

Overview

The central finding is that Lao / Isan people were not peripheral to Siamese slavery. They were structurally important to crown labor, Bangkok construction, specialized urban production, and the transition from slavery to bureaucratically managed labor.

Rather than treating slavery as a narrow legal category, the project analyzes it as a layered labor regime that combined debt bondage, hereditary status, war-captive incorporation, corvée substitution, and administrative classification.

Selected Findings

  • Plausible mid-century slave-stock estimates range from roughly 1.12 million to 2.10 million.
  • Lao accounted for 43.5% of Bowring’s listed royal war-captive fighting men.
  • A central dependent-inclusive Lao captive estimate reaches roughly 80,000.
  • The detailed 1904 census covered only 49.5% of the kingdom.
  • Udon and Isan alone accounted for 22.3% of the national population while being omitted from the detailed frame.
  • A 100-tical debt under the 1905 abolition law still implied about 25 months to freedom.
  • The Ministry of Local Government budget grew by roughly 51 times from 1894–95 to 1903–04.

Methods

The project is built around a long-form historical observation table rather than a single aggregate spreadsheet. Each row records one coded observation: a population count, price, wage, budget entry, legal rule, bounded claim, or coded qualitative fact. This makes it possible to combine diplomatic accounts, census materials, legal texts, and later scholarship without flattening the differences between them.

A key distinction in the analysis is between direct source values and inferred values. Reported figures are kept separate from transparent reconstructions, such as dependent-inclusive captive estimates derived from population benchmarks.

Why this project matters

For portfolio purposes, the contribution is both substantive and methodological. Substantively, it argues that slavery in Siam was a large, multiform system of labor dependence that helped sustain a Bangkok-centered political order. Methodologically, it shows how fragmentary, elite-authored, and politically structured historical evidence can be transformed into a linked dataset, analyzed statistically, and presented as a rigorous economic-history argument.
